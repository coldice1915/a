[TL;DR] What's James Han's story?

    His dad has worked in IT, so naturally, the home he grew up in was filled with electronic devices. When he was 6, together with his older brother they discovered a responsive blank screen on top of an IBM desktop. Every key that was pressed on the keyboard would magically transfer onto the screen. As rewarding as that sounds, they were taught how to boot that desktop with a floppy disc and learned to play their first game on a pc, Dune 2000. Fast forward a few years, and a game called Starcraft came into the picture. Virtually obsessed with the game, James and his brother would wait for the lights to turn off just to play a few local games. There were days when Starcraft didn’t perform as it should and days when they were unable to play on LAN together. It was instances like these that got him thinking, "it's supposed to be working, it always has. What is going on?"

    Expecting something to work and looking forward to it is completely normal. In fact, it's too normal that when the results do not meet the expectation, it springs confusion and hidden stress. This was also known as "problem". People have two methods when solving an unknown problem: first, to figure it out with one's current knowledge and know-hows, or second, to seek knowledge and direction from true sources outside of oneself. When choosing the first, logically one can solve a known problem easily, but one cannot solve an unknown problem which they do not have current knowledge or means to solve the problem with, and in the end, one is left with no newfound skill. Therefore, choosing the latter will almost always end up in the problem being solved, or at least gaining or solidifying some significant knowledge along the way.

    Relationships between people are more significant than any other relationships in this world. Why that is, was the question at hand and James researched further into this phenomena at Binghamton University, focusing on neuroscience to try to understand the psychological and the biological mechanisms of why people are intrinsically drawn to each other, and qualify the value of every person thereof. James' conclusion came to be, that we were designed to be "one" with each other. Meaning that each individual has been pragmatically and genetically coded to belong to and be something greater, outside of our own selves. The practicality of how to optimally fulfill that quest is what James aims to discover.

    It also wasn’t until much later that James came to realize that people are drawn to computers in a similar way people are drawn to other people. One reason could be the necessity of a computer to connect two people that are physically apart from each other. Another reason could be that computers are becoming more anthropomorphous, taking the place of humans in terms of value and relational efficiency. With advances in general technology and artificial intelligence, this discovered phenomena is of no surprise. James has begun to continue his education surrounding computers to be aware and stay ahead of this new virtual reality and its ubiquitous impact on every life today.

    James Han: "I love to be around others and making people feel loved and at peace because that's all that matters in the end. One simple problem that I ran into, and later realized affected every relationship I've been in, was misunderstandings and miscommunications. Change starts from me, and I've resolved to develop clear and consistent communication with those around me within the context of peace."
